Growth Mindset

(by Tarun from DeepThought Edutech Ventures Private Limited)

I was doing a project at IIT Hyderabad, on Graph Theory in the year 2011. I had to read a research paper on Graph Kernels. The paper seemed to have more Greek symbols than English in it, with a lot of notation. I kept procrastinating and I messed up my first formal research project, which I got through the Indian Academy of Sciences Fellowship. My guide Prof. Panduranga Rao gave me very important advice which I use throughout my career. He said, "this is just an abuse of notation, the idea is very simple". I didn't take the advice very seriously when he had first offered it.

However, I gradually understood what "abuse of notation" meant. They use the same notation for two different but similar ideas, this brings a kind of beauty to the way the ideas are expressed. However, the ambiguity makes it difficult for a beginner to understand the notation. The beginner may just give up at a premature stage. So it becomes important to remind yourself that notation would get easier eventually and persevere in the beginning. The initial learning curve would be steep. However, many youngsters just succumb to this and many careers go downhill. Does this have a solution?

The Sanskrit language has a word called Shraddha. This loosely translates to faith or purpose or drive. But Shraddha in reality becomes a faith that the universe can be decoded. If someone has a belief that you can decode the universe, the person observes nature like how Newton did. Otherwise, the person would just give up and say nature is too complicated to understand. As Feynman said, Nature is like chess, we are here to observe the moves and guess the rules of the game. If we are patient, we may eventually decode the game. However, you may end up with moves like castling which are complex and can confuse us. Simple moves like the steps of a pawn may be easy to understand. So do you have Shraddha in you?

Let's discuss something simple. If you are in a class where the teacher is using a lot of new words or new logic, do you give up? Or do you imbibe Shraddha in yourself- that this is decodable? If you have the Shraddha wouldn't you eventually connect the dots and understand what is explained? People with Shraddha grow very fast, while those without Shraddha give up and decide to learn it later. The "later" never comes becomes another priority comes up. The ones with Shraddha workout their brains like how bodybuilders exercise their muscles in a gym. They ask questions and they try to make sense of the idea.

There is a modern idea that comes close to the idea of Shraddha. Carol Dweck's research about the Growth Mindset influenced Satya Nadella. He has been making active attempts to ingrain a Growth Mindset in the culture of Microsoft. Several organizations have been leveraging the power of this concept. DeepThought has been working on a Growth Mindset since its inception in 2020. We look for a growth mindset at the level of recruitment itself. We believe that some youngsters have this grit, humility, and growth mindset due to the parenting and mentoring that they have already received. The candidate could be hailing from a small town in India, but the person may have developed a growth mindset due to good parenting.

In the 21st-century work environment, there would be more areas that you would not know that the areas that you know. So growth mindset becomes important. Innovation environments tend to be chaotic, in fact, chaos drives innovation. So there is a steep learning curve before one can contribute- the initial journey would be very tough before one can get the breakthrough. If you had taken any DeepThought selection process and if you're feeling psychologically drained, this may be an invitation to work on your growth mindset.

Life gives us enough chances to improve ourselves. Growth Mindset and Shraddha may be qualities that you'd like to imbibe in yourself, to accelerate your learning and your career.

The author Tarun is a TEDx speaker, Computer Scientist, IISER Pune alumnus and an Emotional Intelligence coach. He enjoys his work on human capital development- making many human dreams take off.
